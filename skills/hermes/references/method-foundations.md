# HERMES Method Foundations

HERMES is the outcome-oriented project and program management method of the Swiss Confederation (HERMES 2022 / HERMES online), and this reference covers its philosophy, two development approaches, phase model, modules, scenarios, role levels, and interfaces.

## What HERMES is and the outcome-oriented philosophy

HERMES project management is the holistic management method for carrying out projects and programs of various types across many fields of activity, such as organization adjustment, IT, and service and product development. It supports the steering, management, and execution of projects and accompanies the further development of organizational structures, products and services, IT and logistics systems, and infrastructures at varying levels of complexity.

HERMES is an outcome-oriented process method: the focus is on outcomes, not on activity for its own sake. Each module produces a set of essential outcomes along the phases, and the method tracks the rough dependencies and interrelationships between them. Outcomes are at the heart of the method. There is an outcome description for each outcome; documents have document templates that describe their content in more detail; and tasks and roles are assigned to each outcome, so the roles indicate responsibility for and involvement in producing it. HERMES also defines a set of minimum required documents (outcomes) needed to meet project governance requirements.

As a method, HERMES has a clear, easy-to-understand structure with common terminology for all participants, a modular design that can be expanded, and continuous further development. A project can be divided into subprojects that deal with different aspects of the same project (for example subprojects for users, creators, and operators, or for organization, IT, and legal bases). Long-term or complex projects do not have to be structured as programs: they can be run as projects with implementation units.

To keep the method complete, the full scope of HERMES is designed for large, highly complex projects. Because that does not fit every project, HERMES online adapts the standard scenarios in two steps. First, the sizing function determines a project value from several user-entered parameters and provides a scenario scaled to that value. Second, the resulting scenario can optionally be further customized with the tailoring function. The customized scenario can then be downloaded together with the adapted structure plans and templates.

## The two approaches: traditional vs agile

HERMES supports two development approaches and provides a uniform framework that lets either be embedded along with its corresponding project-specific methods:

- Traditional: the phased approach described in systems engineering (Systems Engineering, ETHZ, Walter F. Daenzer). Work proceeds through ordered phases.
- Agile: the iterative and incremental approach, based for example on Extreme Programming or Scrum. In HERMES, agile implementation refers to developing solutions in time-limited projects using both simple agile methods (Scrum, Kanban, and similar) and scaled agile methods (SAFe up to the Essential or ART Flow level).

The choice between them is made during the initiation phase as part of selecting the solution option. The decision must be justified in terms of subject matter and process and should not simply follow current trends; it is derived from the project's premises and shaped by the selected solution option. Within a single program, some projects may run traditionally and others agilely.

The two approaches differ in their understanding of the execution level. Under the traditional approach the project manager issues work orders. Under the agile approach the work of the execution organization is steered by the user representative via the solution requirements, and the teams organize their work independently; the project manager manages the project but must not interfere with the self-organization of the agile execution organization, with the user representative acting as the contact person.

HERMES treats a specific agile development method as a black box inserted into its framework. It does not detail the encapsulated development approach but defines the relevant interfaces for steering, management, communication, and reporting, namely the corresponding outcomes and specific roles. Agile terminology inside the black box is not prescribed; only the outcome interfaces and the project management terminology are defined. This makes the selected project approach autonomous so it can be integrated into any organization, while planning and controlling are done largely the same way regardless of approach. (Further detail is in the separate "Agile Guide - Project management".)

## The phase model in detail

The HERMES phase model is the backbone of every project. It builds on the project life cycle (project start, solution development, project end) and creates a common understanding among all participants for cross-organizational handling of projects. The phases determine the project structure.

Projects are carried out in either five phases (traditional) or three phases (agile). The initiation phase at the start and the closure phase at the end are common to both approaches and bracket the solution development phases.

Traditional approach (5 phases):

- Initiation: a structured orientation for a focused project. It elaborates, compares, and evaluates the project-specific foundations and possible solution options, formulating which solutions exist and which path to take. The choice of solution option includes the decision on whether development will be traditional or agile. It ends with the decision on execution release and the signing of the execution order.
- Concept: the first solution development phase. Requirements and concepts are established (for example organizational requirements, solution requirements, and the various concepts and the solution architecture).
- Implementation: the solution is realized (organization, product, system, interfaces, tests, migration procedure, operating organization, ISDP measures).
- Deployment: the solution is activated and put into operation (organization, product, and system activated; deployment measures and migration carried out; operation activated; ISDP concept transferred; acceptance).
- Closure: the last phase, which conclusively completes the project. Attention focuses on checking, supplementing, and structuring the project documentation, especially formally. It sets out the organizational and administrative transition from the project organization to the application organization, deactivates or removes legacy systems, archives all project data per core-organization rules, and, where necessary, transfers responsibility for the solution.

Agile approach (3 phases): Initiation, then a single Execution phase, then Closure. In agile development, change is fundamental: the execution organization follows the desired impact and proactively responds to changing requirements instead of following a fixed plan, developing and deploying iteratively and incrementally. A phase structure does not make sense here, so the execution phase cannot be subdivided. The execution phase covers the agile development process and serves to embed any agile development method within the HERMES framework. From a project management perspective the initiation phase is always handled traditionally (agile tools may still be used), and interfaces to the core organization and the documents required at project closure remain largely the same as in the traditional approach.

Phases are bounded by milestones that act as quality gates. Milestones sit at the beginning and end of each phase. At a quality gate the outcomes and the procedure are decided, compliance with requirements is checked, and conformity with the strategic objectives and requirements of the core organization is confirmed; achievement of sustainability goals is also taken into account (for example at the next steps milestone). With each phase release the sponsor releases the financial, personnel, and infrastructure resources for the next phase. Under the agile approach, optional interim releases may be approved during the execution phase, creating additional milestones. All milestones are outcomes that mark decision points; each decision task ends with a milestone, and depending on the module there are different decisions and further milestones.

## The modules

Modules are reusable building blocks assigned to phases for creating projects and scenarios. A module groups thematically related outcomes together with the tasks that produce them. Project managers can create additional modules and integrate them into user-defined scenarios. HERMES groups its modules into steering and management modules, execution modules, and a phase-independent module.

Steering and management modules:

- Project steering: ensures the overall, cross-organizational steering of the project and that the set objectives are achieved (initiation, continuous steering, decisions, closure, and early discontinuation where necessary).
- Project management: encompasses the planning, management, and coordination of the project to achieve the outcome and procedure objectives, including all necessary accompanying measures (stakeholders, risks, problems, change management, quality assurance, goods/services).

Execution modules:

- Project foundations: creates a concrete, well-founded starting point for solution development and subsequent closure (study, legal basis analysis, protection needs analysis, procurement analysis, and the prerequisites for the project management plan and execution order).
- Procurement: targeted procurement of a system, product, or service available on the market by means of an open or selective procedure and public tender (other procurement is handled in the project management module).
- Organization: supports the solution-specific structuring or adaptation of the organization and its implementation, providing the organizational and specialist basis for the solution (business model, organizational and process structure).
- Product: designs the product concept and develops or adapts the product, refining the solution requirements and creating detailed specifications.
- IT system: used to develop a system, including the solution architecture, detailed specifications, realization, and integration.
- Tests: systematically and efficiently organizes and conducts solution tests, including realizing and transferring the test infrastructure.
- Deployment organization: supports training and deployment of the new solution or transition to the new state, including the deployment concept, deployment measures, acceptance, and organizational change management.
- IT migration: transfers the data to the new system and decommissions and removes the legacy system, including migration design, execution, and acceptance.
- IT operation: designs and realizes the operating organization at the operator and activates operation, integrating the system into operation.
- ISDP (information security and data protection): defines the necessary information security and data protection measures for the use and operation of the IT solution, including identifying requirements, assessing risks, and designing and implementing measures.

Phase-independent module:

- Situation analysis: a phase-independent outcome used in several modules (for example to establish organizational requirements, prepare solution requirements, and prepare the solution architecture). It recurs across phases rather than belonging to a single one.

## Scenarios and how modules compose into them

A scenario is geared toward implementing projects of a specific nature. It contains precisely those HERMES method components (the relevant modules assigned to phases) that matter for developing that kind of solution, which makes HERMES quick and easy to use. HERMES offers a selection of standard scenarios, for example for an organizational adjustment or for the development of a service/product, and users can adapt standard scenarios to create their own user-defined scenarios.

In the initiation phase the project manager selects the suitable solution option together with the scenario suitable for the development procedure, then plans the specific procedure and the development of the solution on that basis. The project foundations module and parts of other modules are used outside the core solution development, while the remaining modules are enumerated and graphically represented along the solution development of the scenario. Sizing then tailoring (see above) scale and customize the chosen scenario. The full catalog of scenarios and their module-to-scenario mappings is documented separately.

## The three role levels

HERMES distinguishes between roles of the core organization and roles of the project organization but describes only the HERMES roles of the project organization. Each role has a role description covering responsibilities, powers, required skills, and relationships, and each role is assigned to one of three hierarchy levels:

- Steering: sets direction and bears overall responsibility. The minimum steering role is the project sponsor, who steers the project, holds overall responsibility for the project and for achievement of the project objectives, and releases resources at the phase releases (quality gates).
- Management: manages and coordinates the project and determines its course. The minimum management role is the project management, which manages and coordinates the project and sets its course.
- Execution: develops the solution. The minimum execution role is the user representative, who is responsible for solution development; under the agile approach the user representative also steers the execution organization via the solution requirements and is the contact person for the project manager.

Roles are also assigned to one or more partner groups: users, creators, and operators. Different roles are defined and used as needed, but the three roles highlighted as the minimum to be filled are project sponsor, project management, and user representative. These three are the indispensable roles both for the functioning of the project interfaces and for the project as a whole.

## Interfaces and the positioning of program management

HERMES covers the entire project life cycle and is outcome-oriented, guaranteeing compatible standardized interfaces within the project and with the core organization (for example reporting), regardless of whether development is traditional or agile. Its common terminology provides a shared language and understanding between the core and project organizations, between the project and the program, and between project, application, and portfolio management. Within the project organization, the project sponsor, project management, and user representative are the indispensable roles for the interfaces. The first and last phases (initiation and closure) are always common to all projects, which keeps the interfaces to the core organization, controlling, program, and portfolio uniform and channels the transitions to the application organization and operating organization in a uniform way.

HERMES also positions itself relative to neighboring disciplines. It channels knowledge from different areas (project management expertise, agile expertise, governance, sustainability), enhances it with HERMES-specific elements and terminology, and provides a homogeneous framework supported by manuals/online resources, document templates, and training/certification. It requires other relevant methodological training or at least sound project practice. The preface stresses early involvement of architecture, the internal control system (ICS), and security officers, and sufficient automated test cases for internal controls and security elements.

Program management is an extension of project management for organizations with far-reaching, comprehensive changes that need a holistic management system to achieve objectives across a group of interrelated projects in a lean and coordinated way. In program management, projects are grouped together as part of a program. A project can be stand-alone or part of a program; a program contains several projects; and projects and programs can be combined within a portfolio and managed side by side in a core organization. HERMES creates a common understanding of project and program management, provided the project partners involved have the skills to perform their roles. The extension of project management by program management is covered in the appendix to the reference manual.

## How the pieces fit

HERMES lays a uniform framework over either a traditional or an agile approach. Modules (reusable bundles of related outcomes and tasks) are assigned to phases and composed into scenarios for a given project type; sizing scales the chosen scenario to the project value and tailoring customizes it further. The phase model gives every project the same backbone (initiation and closure always present, solution development in between), with milestone quality gates where the steering level releases resources and confirms alignment with the core organization. The three role levels (steering, management, execution) act through their minimum roles (project sponsor, project management, user representative), and outcomes plus common terminology form the standardized interfaces that keep projects, programs, and portfolios coherent regardless of approach.
